Accessibility Guide for Online Meetings

Best practices, recommendations, and resources for making your virtual meetings accessible and inclusive for everyone.

Introduction

As remote and hybrid work environments become the norm, ensuring that online meetings are accessible for all participants is not just a nice-to-have feature‚Äîit's essential. This guide provides practical advice for making your virtual meetings more accessible, with a focus on supporting participants with hearing impairments as well as other accessibility needs.

The recommendations in this guide apply to meetings on any platform, including Google Meet, Microsoft Teams, Zoom, and others. Many of these suggestions are helpful not only for participants with disabilities but for everyone involved in the meeting.

Why Accessibility Matters

Approximately 15% of the global population lives with some form of disability. In the context of online meetings, these can include:

  • Hearing impairments (from mild hearing loss to complete deafness)
  • Visual impairments (from low vision to blindness)
  • Motor disabilities that affect the ability to use traditional input devices
  • Cognitive disabilities that may impact processing information
  • Temporary disabilities (such as a broken arm or ear infection)
  • Situational limitations (noisy environments, poor internet connections)

By making your meetings accessible, you:

  • Ensure all team members can fully participate and contribute
  • Comply with legal accessibility requirements in many jurisdictions
  • Create a more inclusive workplace culture
  • Improve the meeting experience for everyone, including those without disabilities
  • Increase productivity and engagement

üí° Remember

Accessibility isn't about special treatment‚Äîit's about equal access. Many accessibility features, like clear audio, well-structured presentations, and written summaries, benefit all participants.

Before the Meeting

Planning Accessible Meetings

Proper planning is key to ensuring your meetings are accessible from the start:

  1. ‚úì

    Choose accessible platforms

    Select meeting platforms with built-in accessibility features like closed captioning, screen reader compatibility, and keyboard navigation. Google Meet, Microsoft Teams, and Zoom all offer varying levels of accessibility features.

  2. ‚úì

    Schedule with accessibility in mind

    Allow sufficient breaks for longer meetings. Consider time zones and flexibility for participants who may need additional time for setup or assistance.

  3. ‚úì

    Plan for transcription or captioning

    Arrange for live captioning or install AccessibleMeetings to provide real-time transcription. This is essential for participants with hearing impairments.

  4. ‚úì

    Check technical requirements

    Ensure all participants have access to the necessary technology, including any assistive technologies they may use. Offer support or alternative options if needed.

Creating Accessible Invitations

Your meeting invitation is often the first point of contact for participants. Make it accessible by:

  • Using clear, simple language and a logical structure
  • Including all necessary information (date, time, platform, agenda)
  • Providing multiple ways to join (link, phone number, meeting ID)
  • Mentioning available accessibility features (e.g., "Live transcription will be available")
  • Inviting participants to request any additional accommodations they might need
  • Sharing how to test the meeting platform before the actual meeting

Preparing Accessible Materials

Any documents or presentations used during the meeting should be accessible:

  1. ‚úì

    Send materials in advance

    Provide meeting materials at least 24 hours before the meeting so participants can review them at their own pace, especially important for those using screen readers or who need more time to process information.

  2. ‚úì

    Use accessible document formats

    Ensure documents are structured with proper headings, alternative text for images, and accessible formats. Use accessible templates in Word, PowerPoint, or Google Docs.

  3. ‚úì

    Create accessible presentations

    Use high contrast colors, legible fonts (min 18pt), simple backgrounds, and descriptive text. Avoid relying solely on color to convey information.

  4. ‚úì

    Provide alternative formats

    Offer materials in multiple formats (e.g., Word, PDF, HTML) to accommodate different preferences and assistive technologies.

During the Meeting

Starting with Accessible Introductions

How you begin your meeting sets the tone for accessibility:

  • Start with a brief overview of the meeting format and agenda
  • Describe any accessibility features being used (e.g., "We have live transcription running")
  • Ask everyone to introduce themselves before speaking, which helps people with hearing impairments identify speakers
  • Encourage participants to keep video on when possible, which assists those who lip-read
  • Establish clear communication protocols (e.g., how to request to speak, using the chat function)

üí° Pro Tip

Consider asking participants to share their accessibility preferences during introductions. For example, "Please let me know your name, role, and any accessibility preferences you have for this meeting."

Accessible Communication Practices

Clear communication benefits everyone but is essential for accessibility:

  1. ‚úì

    Speak clearly and at a moderate pace

    This improves comprehension for everyone and allows captioning or transcription tools to work more accurately.

  2. ‚úì

    Use plain language

    Avoid unnecessary jargon, acronyms, or complex terminology. When specialized terms must be used, explain them.

  3. ‚úì

    Prevent overlapping speech

    Only one person should speak at a time. This is crucial for accurate transcription and for participants with hearing impairments.

  4. ‚úì

    Face the camera when speaking

    This helps participants who lip-read and generally improves communication clarity.

  5. ‚úì

    Use multiple communication channels

    Encourage the use of chat for questions or comments, which provides an alternative way to participate.

  6. ‚úì

    Check in regularly

    Pause periodically to check if participants have questions or if clarification is needed.

Sharing Accessible Visual Content

When sharing screens or presenting visual information:

  • Verbally describe important visual content for those who cannot see it
  • Ensure text is large enough to be read by all participants
  • Use your mouse pointer or highlighting tools to draw attention to specific content
  • Share presentation files through chat so participants can follow along on their own devices
  • Keep presentations simple and well-organized with consistent navigation
  • Use high contrast color combinations (avoid red/green combinations which are problematic for colorblind participants)

After the Meeting

Accessibility extends beyond the meeting itself. After your meeting:

  1. ‚úì

    Share recordings with captions

    If the meeting was recorded, ensure captions are accurate before sharing. AccessibleMeetings can help by providing a transcript you can reference.

  2. ‚úì

    Distribute accessible meeting notes

    Send out clear, structured notes that summarize key points, decisions, and action items. Use accessible document formats.

  3. ‚úì

    Provide transcripts

    Share meeting transcripts with all participants. With AccessibleMeetings, you can save and distribute these easily.

  4. ‚úì

    Follow up on accessibility issues

    Address any accessibility challenges that arose during the meeting to improve future meetings.

  5. ‚úì

    Gather feedback

    Regularly ask participants if their accessibility needs are being met and how you can improve.

Accessibility Tools Complementary to AccessibleMeetings

While AccessibleMeetings provides real-time transcription, consider these additional tools to enhance accessibility:

  • Screen readers: NVDA (free), JAWS, or VoiceOver (built into macOS) for participants with visual impairments
  • Voice recognition software: Dragon Naturally Speaking or built-in OS options for participants with motor disabilities
  • Color contrast analyzers: To ensure your visual content is readable by all participants
  • Meeting platform native tools: Most platforms have some built-in accessibility features that can be used alongside AccessibleMeetings
